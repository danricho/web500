How to Play 500
The basics

500 is a trick-taking card game for four players in two fixed teams — you and the player sitting opposite are partners. Each hand, one team wins the bidding and commits to a contract : winning at least a stated number of tricks with a trump suit of their choice (or with no trumps at all). Make the contract and your team scores its value; fail and that value is deducted. First team to 500 points wins the game.

The cards

The four-handed game uses a 43-card deck: a standard pack with the 2s, 3s and the black 4s removed, plus one Joker. Red suits run 4 up to Ace; black suits run 5 up to Ace. Each player is dealt 10 cards, and the remaining three go face-down to the middle as the kitty .

Bidding

Starting left of the dealer and moving clockwise, each player either bids or passes. A bid names a number of tricks (6 to 10) and a trump suit or No Trumps — “seven hearts” means our team will win at least seven tricks with hearts as trumps . Every bid must be worth more points than the one before it. For the same number of tricks the suits rank, lowest to highest: [ ♠︎ ♣︎ ♦︎ ♥︎ No Trumps] — so eight spades (240) beats seven no trumps (220). The full table is under BID VALUES on the scoreboard.

Passing knocks you out of the auction for the rest of the hand. When only one bidder is left, they get a single chance to raise their own bid before the contract locks in. If all four players pass, the hand is thrown in and re-dealt.

Misère

Misère is a special bid: a contract to lose every trick, played with no trumps, worth 250 points. It may only be bid once the highest bid so far is a seven, and only a bid worth more than 250 beats it (eight clubs and up — eight spades at 240 does not). The misère bidder plays alone — their partner sits the hand out and tricks are three cards. The contract fails the instant the bidder wins a trick.

Open Misère not implemented is the bolder version, worth 500 points: the same lose-every-trick contract, but played with the bidder’s hand face-up on the table for everyone to see. It cannot be bid in this game yet.

The kitty

The winning bidder picks up the three kitty cards, then discards any three cards face-down, keeping their best ten.

Bowers and the Joker

In a suit contract the top three trumps are, highest first:

  • the Joker ,
  • the right bower — the Jack of the trump suit,
  • the left bower — the Jack of the other suit of the same colour.

Then Ace, King, Queen and so on. The left bower counts as a trump in every way: it belongs to the trump suit for following suit, not the suit printed on it. Non-trump suits rank normally, Ace high (the same-colour suit is simply missing its Jack).

In No Trumps and Misère the Joker is the only trump — it wins any trick it is played to, but it may only be played when you cannot follow suit. Leading it is special: the leader must nominate a suit (in a panel at the top right) which the other players must follow if they can. The nominated suit must be one that has not been led earlier in the hand — so once all four suits have been led, the Joker cannot be led at all, except to the last trick (where no nomination is needed).

Joker pre-nomination — a contractor who holds the Joker may instead declare its suit before leading to the first trick (offered in the same top-right panel). The Joker then counts as the highest card of that suit for the whole hand: it must be played to follow that suit, it wins any trick of that suit, and it wins nothing when thrown on another suit — which is exactly how a misère bidder can get rid of it. Declaring is optional; just lead a card to skip it.

Playing the hand

The contract winner leads the first trick, and play moves clockwise. You must follow the suit led if you can; otherwise you may play anything — trump in, or throw off a loser. The highest trump in the trick wins it, or the highest card of the led suit if no trumps were played. The trick winner leads the next trick.

Because the Joker and left bower are trumps:

  • if trumps are led and they are your only trumps, you must play one of them;
  • if the left bower is your only card of its printed suit, you are not forced to play it when that suit is led;
  • in misère, if you cannot follow suit and hold the (un-nominated) Joker, you must play it;
  • in No Trumps or misère, leading the Joker means nominating a not-yet-led suit for the others to follow — unless the contractor pre-nominated its suit, in which case it simply is the top card of that suit (see Bowers and the Joker above).

Don’t worry about memorising these — cards you can’t legally play are darkened in your hand.

Scoring

Contract made: the bidding team scores the bid’s value (see BID VALUES). Winning all ten tricks scores at least 250 — the slam rule. Contract failed: the bid’s value is deducted, and scores can go negative. The other team always scores 10 points per trick they win, whether the contract succeeds or not — except against misère, where they score nothing. Misère itself scores plus or minus 250.

Winning the game

The first team to reach 500 points or more wins. A team falling to minus 500 or worse loses immediately — going out the back door .

Limitations / Not in this game (yet)

A few parts of the traditional Australian game are not implemented:

  • Open Misère — the 500-point exposed-hand misère described above cannot be bid.
This summary is based on the Australian four-handed game as described by John McLeod at pagat.com/euchre/500.html . For the definitive treatment and every regional variation, see that page.
